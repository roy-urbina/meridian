🚀 Getting Started — Your First Day in Meridian
Where do I begin?
Follow this order and you will have a fully working financial picture in about 15 minutes:

Step 1 — Set up your Accounts. Go to the Accounts page and add every real bank account, credit card, and loan you have. For each one set the correct type (checking, savings, credit, loan) and enter the current balance as the Starting Balance. This is the foundation everything else builds on.

Step 2 — Set up your Budget categories. Go to the Budget page. Your categories are already pre-loaded with the full chart of accounts (0110 through 0295). Fill in the default monthly amount for categories you actually use and leave the rest at $0.

Step 3 — Set up your Funds. Go to the Funds page and create funds for money you are setting aside — Emergency Fund, Gas, Groceries, Car Maintenance, etc. Optionally link each fund to a budget category so transactions auto-populate.

Step 4 — Start entering transactions. Go to Transactions and start entering what you spend and earn. Tag the fund and category on each one. Within a week you will have enough data for the dashboard to be meaningful.

Step 5 — Set up Recurring. Add your regular bills and your paycheck as recurring transactions so Meridian reminds you when they are due.
What is the overall philosophy of Meridian?
Meridian combines two approaches: ledger accounting (every dollar that comes in or goes out is recorded) and envelope budgeting (every dollar is assigned a purpose). Your Accounts page tracks where your money physically is. Your Funds page tracks what your money is earmarked for. Your Budget page tracks what you planned to spend vs what you actually spent. These three views of your money work together — they should always tell the same story from different angles.
What is stored and where?
Everything — all accounts, transactions, funds, budgets, templates, recurring transactions, and rules — is stored in your browser's localStorage. It never leaves your device. There is no account, no server, no sync. The app is a single HTML file. If you clear your browser data you will lose everything, so back up the file periodically by saving a copy of it to a safe location.
💡 Pro tip: The first time you open the Yearly Budget tab, your current account balance is locked as the Anchor Balance. Make sure you have entered all your accounts and their correct starting balances before you open that tab for the first time.

Add a new transaction row — works from any page, not just Transactions. Useful when you just paid for something and want to record it immediately without navigating first.
T
Open the Templates panel when on the Transactions page. Slide it open, pick a template, and hit Apply in under 2 seconds.
/
Focus the global search bar in the sidebar instantly. Start typing a payee, category code, fund name, or account name and results appear immediately.
Escape
Close any open modal, the Templates panel, or search results. Also cancels a cell edit without saving.
Enter
Confirm a cell edit and blur the field. In autocomplete dropdowns, selects the highlighted option.
Tab
Move forward to the next editable cell in a table row. Shift+Tab moves backward.
↑ ↓
Navigate through autocomplete suggestions (payee and category fields) and global search results.
Shortcuts only activate when you are not currently typing inside a cell, input, or contenteditable field. If a shortcut is not working, click somewhere neutral on the page first to deselect any active input.
🏦 Accounts
What types of accounts should I create?
Create one account for every real-world financial account you want to track:

Checking — your day-to-day spending account. This is where most transactions will come from.
Savings — your savings account. Transactions here are usually transfers in and out plus interest.
Cash — physical cash you carry. Use this if you want to track cash spending. When you withdraw from ATM, record a transfer from Checking to Cash.
Credit — credit cards. These start with a negative balance (or zero if you have no balance). Purchases are payments, payments to the card are deposits.
Loan — student loans, car loans, mortgages. The balance is negative (you owe money). Loan payments reduce the balance over time.

Example: Roy has Bank of America Checking, Bank of America Savings, a cash envelope, a Capital One credit card, and a Navient student loan. That is 5 accounts in Meridian.
What is Starting Balance and how do I set it correctly?
The Starting Balance is the account balance on the day you start using Meridian. Every transaction you enter after that date will adjust the balance from this starting point.

For checking/savings: Open your bank app, find your current balance, enter it as a positive number. Example: $2,450.00.
For credit cards: Enter the current balance as a negative number since you owe money. Example: if you owe $350 on your card, enter -350.
For loans: Same as credit — enter the outstanding principal as a negative number. Example: -18,500 for a student loan.
For cash: Count your cash and enter it as a positive number.

Important: do not enter transactions that already happened before your starting date. The starting balance already accounts for all of that history. Only enter transactions from today forward.
What does the Goal column on Accounts do?
For asset accounts (checking, savings), the Goal is a target balance you want to maintain or reach. The progress bar shows how close you are. For debt accounts (credit, loan), set the goal to $0 — the progress bar then shows how much of the debt you have paid off. This is purely for your reference; it does not affect any calculations.
How does net worth work?
Net worth = Total Assets minus Total Liabilities. Assets are all checking, savings, and cash accounts with positive balances. Liabilities are credit cards and loans. Meridian calculates this automatically and shows it on the Dashboard and Accounts page. Watch this number grow over time — it is the single most important financial metric.
↕️ Transactions
How do I enter a transaction?
Press N or click ＋ Add Row. A new row appears at the bottom of the table. Click each cell to edit it:

1. Account — which real bank account this transaction affects
2. Date — the date it occurred (defaults to today)
3. Num — auto-filled with the next number, but you can override with a check number, "DD" for direct deposit, "ATM" for ATM withdrawal, "ACH", etc.
4. Payee — who you paid or who paid you. Start typing and past payees appear as suggestions.
5. Memo — optional note about the transaction
6. Fund — which envelope this money belongs to (optional but recommended)
7. Category — the budget category code, e.g. "0261 - Groceries". Start typing the code or name and matches appear.
8. Payment — amount going OUT of the account (expenses, bills, purchases)
9. Deposit — amount coming INTO the account (income, refunds, transfers in)

Example: You bought groceries at Whole Foods for $87.32 on your checking account.
Account: Bank of America Checking | Date: today | Payee: Whole Foods | Fund: Groceries | Category: 0261 - Groceries | Payment: 87.32
What is the difference between Payment and Deposit?
Think of it from the account's perspective. Payment is money leaving the account — you spent it. Deposit is money entering the account — you received it. Never put a negative number in either field; use the correct column instead. For a paycheck: Deposit $2,000. For rent: Payment $1,200. For a refund: Deposit the refund amount.
What does the Clr column mean and when should I use it?
Clr stands for Cleared status. Click the badge on any row to cycle through the three states:

— (blank) — Uncleared. You entered this transaction but have not verified it against your bank statement yet. Use this for transactions you just entered that have not posted to your bank yet.
C — Cleared — This transaction has appeared on your bank statement or online account and you have confirmed the amount is correct.
R — Reconciled — This transaction was marked during a formal reconciliation session and the account balance matched perfectly. This is the highest level of verification.

Good habit: when you receive your monthly statement, go through each transaction and mark them C. Then run a full reconciliation to mark them R.
How does the payee autocomplete work?
When you click or type in the Payee field, a dropdown shows all payees you have used before, filtered by what you are typing. The matching text is highlighted in blue. Use ↑↓ arrows to navigate, Enter or Tab to select. The list is built from all your existing transactions, so the more you use Meridian the smarter it gets.
How does the category typeahead work?
The Category field is a text input, not a dropdown. Start typing either the code number or the name and matching categories appear. For example:

Type "026" → shows all Personal & Lifestyle categories (0260 through 0269)
Type "groc" → shows 0261 - Groceries
Type "0261" → goes directly to 0261 - Groceries

Press Tab to accept the top match and move to the next field. This is the fastest way to categorize transactions.
How do I record a transfer between my own accounts?
Click ⇄ Add transfer at the bottom of the table. In the modal, select the From account and To account, enter the amount and date, and optionally add a memo. Meridian creates both legs automatically — a Payment on the From account and a Deposit on the To account — and links them together so deleting one would not leave an orphan.

Example: Moving $500 from Checking to Savings.
From: Bank of America Checking | To: Bank of America Savings | Amount: 500 | Date: today
Result: Checking shows -$500, Savings shows +$500. Net worth unchanged.
What is an intra-fund transfer and when do I use it?
An intra-fund transfer is a real-account transfer where you also tag a fund — meaning the money physically moved between accounts but is still earmarked for the same purpose.

Example: Your Gas fund has $100 total — $50 backed by Checking, $50 backed by Savings. You withdraw $30 cash from the ATM to pay for gas in cash. Record a transfer from Checking to Cash, and tag the Gas fund on it. Now:
— Gas fund total: still $100 (the $30 just moved from Checking to Cash, both are yours)
— Gas fund By Account: Checking $20, Savings $50, Cash $30
— Gas fund Spent: still $0 (nothing was actually spent yet)

When you actually pay for gas with that cash, record a Payment from the Cash account tagged to Gas. Then Spent shows $30.

When you tag a fund on a transfer leg, Meridian automatically asks if you want to sync the other leg to the same fund. Usually yes — unless you are intentionally doing a manual fund-to-fund reallocation via real accounts.
How do I filter and search transactions?
The filter bar above the table has several tools that all work together:

Account dropdown — show only one account
Date From / Date To — show a date range. Your selections are remembered between sessions.
Search box — searches payee, memo, category, account name, date, and exact amount. Type "87.32" to find that specific amount. Type "whole foods" to find all Whole Foods transactions.
Min $ / Max $ — filter by transaction size. Set Min to 500 to see all large transactions. Set both to find transactions in a specific range.
Clear button — resets all filters at once.

The 🔍 Find Similar button on each row is a shortcut that filters to all transactions with the same payee instantly.
💡 The subtitle under "Transactions" in the page header shows how many transactions match your current filters. If it says "0 matching transactions" you have conflicting filters — click Clear to start over.
💼 Funds — Envelope Budgeting
What exactly is a fund and why should I use it?
A fund is a virtual envelope — a named bucket that represents a portion of the money you already have in your real accounts. The total of all your fund balances should ideally equal your total assets, meaning every dollar you have is assigned a purpose.

Without funds, you might have $3,000 in checking and not know: how much is for rent, how much is for groceries, how much is for the car repair coming up. With funds, you always know. $1,200 is in Rent. $400 is in Groceries. $500 is in Car Maintenance. The remaining $900 has not been assigned yet (shown as "Unassigned" in the fund balance check).

Think of it like the old cash envelope system — you cash your paycheck, put $200 in the grocery envelope, $150 in the gas envelope, $400 in the rent envelope. When the grocery envelope is empty, you stop buying groceries (or move money from another envelope). Meridian does this digitally.
How does the fund balance get calculated?
A fund's balance is the sum of three things:

1. Tagged transactions — any transaction where you set the Fund field to this fund. Deposits add to the balance, payments reduce it.
2. Category-linked transactions — if the fund has a Linked Category set, any transaction with that category automatically counts toward the fund (shown with an amber AUTO badge).
3. Contributions — explicit allocation entries you make via Fund Actions → Contribute to Fund.

Intra-fund transfers (transfers between real accounts with a fund tag) net to zero — they shift which account backs the money but do not change the fund total.
Walk me through a real example of using funds with multiple accounts.
Scenario: Roy gets paid $2,000 into Checking. He wants to allocate it across his funds.

1. The paycheck deposit is posted to Checking — tagged as 0110 - Wages & Income.
2. Auto-replenishment rules fire: 20% → Emergency Fund ($400), 15% → Rent ($300), 10% → Groceries ($200), 5% → Gas ($100). The preview shows the split. Roy clicks Apply.
3. Four contribution entries are created, each attributed to the Checking account.
4. Now the Emergency Fund shows: Checking $400 in the By Account pills. Gas fund: Checking $100.
5. Roy later pays $87 at Whole Foods. He tags: Account = Checking, Fund = Groceries, Category = 0261 - Groceries.
6. Groceries fund now shows: balance $113 ($200 contributed minus $87 spent), Checking $113.
7. He also moves $50 cash from Checking for gas purchases. He records the transfer (Checking → Cash) and tags Fund = Gas. Now Gas fund shows: Checking $50, Cash $50.
8. He pays $48 at Shell from Cash. Tags Account = Cash, Fund = Gas, Category = 0252 - Gas (Routine).
9. Gas fund: Checking $50, Cash $2. Total $52. Spent $48.
What is a Linked Category and how do I set it up?
Each fund has a Category field in the fund table. Type in a budget category name — for example, type "0252 - Gas (Routine)" in the Category column of your Gas fund. From that point on, any transaction you categorize as "0252 - Gas (Routine)" will automatically count toward the Gas fund balance, even if you forget to set the Fund field on the transaction. You will see a small count badge (e.g. "3 auto") showing how many transactions are currently being auto-linked. This is a huge time-saver for consistent categories.
How do I see the full history of a fund?
Click the ▶ expand button on the right side of any fund row. The history expands inline below that row — no page navigation needed. You will see:

By Account section — pills showing each real account and its net contribution to this fund. This tells you which account the money is actually sitting in.
Fund Transfers section (if any) — virtual fund-to-fund transfers that had no real account attached.
Activity table — every transaction and contribution affecting this fund, in date order, with a running balance column and badges (CONTRIB, AUTO, REALLOC, XFER) identifying what each entry is.

Only one fund can be expanded at a time. Click ▶ on another fund to switch, or click ▼ on the current fund to collapse it.
What are auto-replenishment rules?
Rules that automatically split incoming deposits across your funds by percentage. Set them up under Fund Actions → ⚡ Auto-Replenishment Rules.

The modal has a two-panel layout: left panel lists all accounts you have configured rules for, right panel is the editor for the selected account. To add a new account, use the dropdown at the bottom of the left panel. Each account has its own independent set of rules — your Checking account and your Savings account can have completely different splits.

Example rules for Checking: Emergency Fund 20%, Rent 30%, Groceries 15%, Gas 10%, Entertainment 5%. Total = 80%. The remaining 20% stays unallocated in the account.

Rules trigger when: (1) you type a deposit amount into a transaction for the first time, (2) you post a recurring deposit, or (3) you apply a deposit template. A preview modal shows the exact dollar amounts before applying. Click "Skip This Time" if you do not want to run it for a particular deposit.
🎯 Budget — Monthly and Yearly Views
What is the difference between the Monthly and Yearly tabs?
They show the same data in different ways. The Monthly tab is for detailed month-by-month review — you see budget vs actual for a single month with progress bars and difference columns. The Yearly tab is for planning and the big picture — all 12 months in one grid so you can see seasonal patterns, set different amounts for different months, and track the cumulative projection.

Use Monthly for: reviewing how you did last month, seeing which categories are over budget right now.
Use Yearly for: setting your annual budget plan, checking whether your projected year-end balance is healthy, seeing which months will be tight.
How do the 4-digit category codes work?
The codes create a hierarchy that Meridian uses to group and roll up data automatically:

First digit: 0 = all categories
Second digit: 1 = Income, 2 = Expenses
Third digit: the group (1 = Earned Income, 2 = Military Pay, 3 = Other Income for income; 1 = Housing, 2 = Utilities, 3 = Debt, etc. for expenses)
Fourth digit: the specific category within the group

So 0121 - NG Allowance means: expenses/income (0), income (1), Military Pay group (2), fourth item (1).
The Yearly budget view shows group rows like "012 - Military Pay" as collapsible subtotal rows — click the triangle to expand or collapse all the categories in that group.

You do not need to memorize the codes. Just type part of the name in the Category field on a transaction and the right category appears. The code is there to keep your data organized and to make the Yearly view's hierarchy work automatically.
How do I set different budget amounts for different months?
In the Yearly tab, make sure the view toggle is set to Budget. Click any cell in the grid — it becomes editable. Type the amount and press Enter or Tab. That amount applies only to that specific month for that category.

Example: Your utility bills are higher in winter. Set 0220 - Utilities: Electricity to $200 for December, January, February and $100 for the summer months. The Monthly tab will show $200 as the budget when you are viewing January.

In the Monthly tab, cells with a monthly override show a small amber dot (●) so you can tell at a glance which amounts are customized for that month vs using the category default.
What are the Budget, Actual, and Difference toggles in the Yearly view?
Budget — shows what you planned to spend/earn per month. Cells are editable. Projected Balance row appears at the bottom.
Actual — shows what you actually spent/earned, pulled live from your transactions. Color coded: green = under budget, amber = close, red = over. End Balance row appears at the bottom.
Difference — Budget minus Actual. Green = under budget (good for expenses), red = over budget. Lets you see your surplus or shortfall per category per month at a glance.
What is the Anchor Balance and why does it matter?
The Anchor Balance is a one-time locked snapshot of your total assets (all non-debt account balances) taken the first time you open the Yearly budget tab. It is shown in the grid with a 🔒 icon and the date it was locked.

It matters because it is the baseline for all projections. Every Projected Balance calculation starts from this number and adds the budgeted NET month by month. If your anchor is wrong (e.g. you had not set up all your accounts yet when it locked), your projections will be off.

Important: make sure all your accounts are set up with correct starting balances before you open the Yearly tab for the first time.

The anchor is intentionally permanent — changing it later would shift all your projections and make historical comparisons meaningless.
Walk me through the Projected Balance row.
Example: Anchor = $5,000. January NET budget = +$800. February NET budget = +$600. March NET budget = -$200 (a big expense month).

January Projected Balance: $5,000 + $800 = $5,800 (green — balance growing)
February Projected Balance: $5,800 + $600 = $6,400 (green)
March Projected Balance: $6,400 + (-$200) = $6,200 (red — balance declined that month)

This tells you: if you stick to your budget perfectly, you should have $6,200 in your accounts at the end of March. Compare this to the End Balance row in Actual view — if End Balance shows $6,150, you overspent by $50 somewhere. This is the cross-check that makes the whole system work.
What is the End Balance row in Actual view?
It shows what your total account balance (non-debt accounts) actually was at the end of each month, calculated directly from your transaction history. Future months show "—" since they have not happened yet. The current month is highlighted with a teal outline.

The most important check: the current month's End Balance should match the Assets card on your Accounts page. If it does, your data is perfectly reconciled. If it does not, there is a discrepancy — a missing transaction, a wrong amount, or a starting balance that was entered incorrectly.
⚖️ Reconciliation — Matching Your Records to Your Bank
What is reconciliation and why does it matter?
Reconciliation is the process of verifying that every transaction in Meridian matches a transaction on your official bank or credit card statement. When you reconcile, you know three things with certainty: (1) you have not missed any transactions, (2) no amounts are wrong, (3) your Meridian balance matches your real balance to the penny.

Most people reconcile once a month when their statement arrives. Banks can occasionally make errors, auto-charges you forgot about can show up, or you might have entered a wrong amount. Reconciliation catches all of this.
Step-by-step: how do I reconcile?
1. Have your bank statement open (online or paper).
2. Go to the Transactions page, click ⚖ Reconcile.
3. Select the account you are reconciling from the dropdown.
4. Enter the Statement Ending Balance — the balance shown at the end of the period on your statement. For a credit card, enter the amount you owe as a positive number (e.g. 350, not -350) — Meridian handles the sign automatically.
5. A list of uncleared transactions for that account appears. Check off each transaction that appears on your statement. The Cleared Balance and Difference update in real time as you check.
6. When Difference = $0.00, the status turns green and says "✓ Balanced — ready to reconcile."
7. Click Mark Reconciled. All checked transactions are stamped R.

If you cannot get to $0.00 difference: look for a transaction on your statement that is not in Meridian (missing entry), a transaction in Meridian that is not on the statement (entered too early or wrong date), or an amount discrepancy (typed $78 but statement shows $87).
What if I cancel partway through?
No problem at all. Clicking Cancel or the X button clears all temporary checkmarks and resets everything. Nothing is saved to your transactions until you click Mark Reconciled. The next time you open reconcile it will start completely fresh with no pre-checked boxes.
What is the difference between Cleared (C) and Reconciled (R)?
Think of Cleared as "I verified this exists on my bank account" and Reconciled as "I verified this as part of a formal balance check and everything added up." Reconciled is a stronger guarantee. In practice: mark things C as you spot-check your account online throughout the month, then run a full reconciliation at month-end to mark everything R and confirm the balance is perfect.
🔁 Recurring Transactions
What should I set up as recurring?
Anything that happens on a predictable schedule:

Bills (payments): Rent/mortgage, Netflix, Spotify, Amazon Prime, gym membership, insurance, loan payments, utilities if they are consistent
Income (deposits): Paycheck (biweekly or monthly), NG drill pay, any regular side income

Do not add things that vary significantly month to month (like groceries or gas) as recurring — those are better handled through regular transaction entry so the actual amounts are tracked accurately.
Walk me through setting up a biweekly paycheck.
1. Go to Recurring, click ＋ Add Recurring.
2. Account: Bank of America Checking
3. Payee: Employer (or your employer's name)
4. Category: 0110 - Wages & Income
5. Type: Deposit
6. Amount: your net pay amount, e.g. 1847.50
7. Frequency: Biweekly
8. Start Date: your next pay date
9. Memo: Bi-weekly paycheck
10. Click Save.

When the pay date arrives, the row turns red with a ● DUE badge. Click Post — the transaction is added to your ledger, dated the due date. If you have auto-replenishment rules set up for Checking, the split preview will appear automatically after posting.
What if a recurring transaction is due but the amount changed?
Click the ✏️ edit icon on that recurring row. Update the amount in the modal, then click Save. The change applies to all future postings. For the current due posting, you can also just post it at the old amount and then manually edit the transaction amount in the ledger afterward if needed.
Can I post all due transactions at once?
Yes — when any transactions are due, a red banner appears at the top of the Recurring page with a Post All Due button. This posts every due transaction at once. If any of those deposits trigger auto-replenishment rules, the preview modal will appear for each one in sequence.
💡 The sidebar shows a red badge on the Recurring nav item whenever transactions are due. This means you will always know even if you never go to the Recurring page.
📈 Reports
What does the Income vs Expenses chart show?
A paired bar chart covering the last 6 months. Teal bars = income, coral bars = expenses. Transfers are excluded from both — only real income and real spending are shown. Hover any bar to see the exact amounts. This chart answers the most important question in personal finance: am I earning more than I am spending? Green trend = growing, red trend = not sustainable.

Below the chart are 4 summary cards: 6-month total income, 6-month total expenses, net (income minus expenses), and spending trend (whether last month was better or worse than the month before it).
What do the account sparklines show?
Each account gets a tiny line chart showing its balance for each of the last 6 months, plus the current balance and the month-over-month change in dollars and percentage. The line color is green if the trend is favorable (growing for assets, shrinking for debts) and red if unfavorable.

This is particularly useful for debt accounts — you can see at a glance if your loan balance is actually going down each month or if payments are barely keeping up with interest.
How do I use the Transaction Statement?
The statement at the bottom of the Reports page is a filterable ledger. Set the Account dropdown to see one account or leave it blank for all. Set a date range. The table shows every transaction with a running balance column — the right-most column shows what the account balance was after each transaction.

The totals row at the bottom shows total payments, total deposits, and net for the period (transfers excluded from totals).

Your date range filters are saved between sessions, so the next time you open Reports the same range is still set.
How do I export a PDF statement?
1. Set your filters in the Transaction Statement section (account, date range).
2. Click ⬇ Export PDF in the top right of the Reports page.
3. A new browser tab opens with a clean, light-mode document — professional layout with a Meridian header, summary cards (net worth, assets, period income, period expenses), account balances table, and the full transaction table.
4. A blue bar at the top shows the suggested filename (e.g. meridian-statement-2026-03-20) and a 🖨 Save as PDF button.
5. Click the button, your browser's print dialog opens. Set the Destination to "Save as PDF" and click Save.

The PDF always uses a light background regardless of your dark mode setting, so it looks clean when printed or shared.
📋 Templates — Save and Reuse Transactions
What is a template?
A template is a saved transaction recipe — all the fields pre-filled so you can create a new transaction with one click. Templates are best for things that happen regularly but not on a fixed schedule, where the amount is always the same or nearly the same.

Good candidates for templates: your landlord's rent payment (same amount every month but you post it manually), a regular cash withdrawal (same amount each time), a split transaction you make every week, a payee you use all the time with the same category and fund.
How do I create a template?
Three ways:

From an existing transaction: Click the 📋 icon on any transaction row. A modal opens pre-filled with all that transaction's details. Give it a name (e.g. "Monthly Rent") and click Save Template. Takes about 5 seconds.

From scratch: Click 📋 Templates in the Transactions header (or press T), then ＋ Create New Template. Fill in the fields manually.

Via duplicate: The ⧉ Duplicate button on each row creates an exact copy of that transaction dated today, already in the ledger. This is not a template — it is a one-time copy. Use it for genuinely ad-hoc recurring entries.
How do I apply a template?
Click 📋 Templates or press T to open the panel. Your templates appear as cards showing the payee, category, type, amount, and account. Click Apply ↗ on any card. Meridian instantly creates a new transaction dated today with all the pre-filled fields, scrolls to it in the ledger, and leaves it selected so you can edit any field if needed (e.g. adjust the amount slightly).
🔍 Search — Finding Anything Instantly
How does the global search bar work?
Press / from anywhere or click the search bar in the sidebar. Type any word or number and results appear grouped by type: Transactions, Accounts, Funds, and Budget Categories. The matching text is highlighted in each result.

Click any result to jump directly to it:
— Transaction results → Transactions page filtered to that payee
— Account results → Transactions page filtered to that account
— Fund results → Funds page
— Budget category results → opens a popup with that category's full breakdown

Use ↑↓ to navigate results, Enter to select, Escape to close.
What does clicking a budget category in search results do?
It opens a category popup showing:

3 stat cards — Budget, Actual, and Remaining for the selected month
Donut chart — visual percentage of budget used, color coded (green = on track, amber = close, red = over)
Breakdown panel — exact budgeted vs actual with a progress bar
6-month sparkbar — small bar chart showing spending in this category for the last 6 months, with a dashed line at the budget amount so you can see which months went over
Transaction list — every transaction in this category for the selected month

Use the month selector in the popup header to see any month without closing the popup. The "Open in Budget →" and "View Transactions →" buttons at the bottom jump to the relevant pages filtered to that category.
What does the 🔍 Find Similar button on a transaction do?
Clicking the 🔍 icon on any transaction row instantly filters the Transactions page to show all transactions with the same payee. This is the fastest way to answer "how much have I spent at this place total?" — click once and you see your entire history with that payee.
💡 Tips, Habits, and Common Mistakes
Best practices for daily use
Enter transactions the same day. The longer you wait, the harder it is to remember what you bought and which category it belongs to. If you buy something, pull out your phone, press N, and enter it on the spot. It takes 20 seconds.

Always set both a Fund and a Category. The Fund tells the envelope system where the money belongs. The Category tells the budget system what kind of spending it was. They often overlap (Gas fund + 0252 - Gas category) but serve different purposes.

Reconcile monthly. When your bank statement arrives or at the end of each month, reconcile every account. It keeps your data clean and catches errors before they compound.
Best practices for the monthly workflow
1. First day of the month: Post any recurring transactions that are due. Review the Dashboard to see last month's actuals.
2. Throughout the month: Enter transactions daily or every few days. Clear (C) transactions as they post to your bank.
3. Last few days: Reconcile all accounts against statements. Review the Budget page for the current month — see which categories are over and adjust spending accordingly.
4. End of month: Switch to the Yearly budget tab and switch to Actual view. Check that the End Balance for the current month matches your Accounts page Assets card. If it does, your books are clean.
Common mistakes to avoid
Entering a transfer as two separate transactions instead of using ⇄ Add Transfer. If you manually enter a payment from Checking and a deposit to Savings as separate unlinked entries, the fund system cannot tell they are the same money moving. Always use the transfer modal for account-to-account moves.

Setting the wrong Starting Balance. If your starting balance is off by $10, every balance calculation in Meridian will be off by $10 forever. Double-check starting balances carefully. If you discover an error, fix the starting balance on the Accounts page — it will immediately correct all derived balances.

Opening the Yearly budget tab before setting up all accounts. The Anchor Balance locks the first time you open the Yearly tab. If accounts are missing or balances are wrong at that point, your projections will be anchored to a bad number.

Ignoring the fund balance check banner. The amber "Unassigned" banner on the Funds page is not an error — it just means you have money in accounts not yet earmarked to a fund. If it turns red ("Over-allocated"), that is a real problem that needs attention.

Using the same category name for very different things. The budget and fund system matches transactions to categories by exact name. If you use "Misc" for everything, none of your budget categories will have meaningful actuals.
Your data safety
Meridian stores everything in your browser's localStorage — it never leaves your device and is never sent to any server. This means:

✅ Complete privacy — no accounts, no tracking, no cloud
✅ Works offline — no internet connection needed after loading
⚠️ Clearing browser data will delete everything — be careful with "Clear browsing data"
⚠️ Data does not sync between devices or browsers automatically

Backup recommendation: Once a week, export a PDF statement from the Reports page as a record. Once a month, save a copy of the HTML file itself (right-click → Save As) to a folder or cloud drive. The file contains the app and your data together.
💡 If something seems off — a balance looks wrong, a fund is showing an unexpected amount — the first thing to check is always the Accounts page. Make sure each account has the correct Starting Balance and that no duplicate transactions exist for the same date and amount.

☁️ Sync Data — GitHub Gist

Backup and sync your Meridian data across devices
How this works: Your data is saved as a private file on your GitHub account (called a Gist). Only you can see it. To sync between devices, open Meridian on each device and paste the same token and Gist ID.

One-time setup: Create a free GitHub account, then generate a personal access token with Gist permissions. Takes about 2 minutes. See the instructions below.
This is stored only in your browser's localStorage and never sent anywhere except GitHub's own API.
📖 Step-by-step setup instructions ▶
Step 1 — Create a GitHub account
Go to github.com and sign up for a free account if you don't have one. Confirm your email.

Step 2 — Generate a Personal Access Token
1. Click your profile picture (top right) → Settings
2. Scroll all the way down → click Developer settings
3. Click Personal access tokens → Tokens (classic)
4. Click Generate new token → Generate new token (classic)
5. Give it a name like "Meridian Sync"
6. Set expiration to "No expiration" (or however long you want)
7. Check the gist checkbox — that's the only permission needed
8. Click Generate token at the bottom
9. Copy the token immediately — GitHub only shows it once. It starts with "ghp_"

Step 3 — Paste the token above and click Push to Cloud
Leave the Gist ID blank on your first sync. Meridian will create a new private Gist automatically and show you the ID to save.

Step 4 — Set up on your other devices
Open Meridian on your phone or another computer. Paste the same token and the Gist ID that was created in Step 3. Click Pull from Cloud to load your data.
